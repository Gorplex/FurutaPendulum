2  * This page describes how to connect a PolyDAQ to various types of sensors.

4  * @section sec_sg Strain Gauges
5  *
6  * @subsection sg_steps Steps
7  * -# Wire up a full, four-resistance Wheatstone bridge. If using a half-bridge
8  * or quarter-bridge circuit, connect dummy gauges or passive resistors at
9  * this time.
10  * -# Before connecting the bridge to the PolyDAQ, check the resistances between
11  * all the corners of the bridge. These should be @c R between opposite corners
12  * and @c 3/4 @c R between adjacent corners.
13  * -# Connect the bridge to the @c VEX, @c Sn+, @c Sn-, and @c GND terminals of
14  * one bridge amplifier.
15  * -# Balance the strain gauge bridge so that it gives a known reading, usually
16  * half the maximum full-scale output, at zero strain.
17  * - @b Auto-balanced (strain channels 1 and 2):
18  * - Using a dumb terminal, issue the @b L command for channel @c S1 or the
19  * @b M command for channel @c S2. This runs the auto-balancer.
20  * - You should receive a message saying @"@c Strain @c bridge @e ch
21  * @c balanced @c to @e number@" where @e ch is channel 1 or 2 and @e n is
22  * the A/D output
23  * for zero strain, usually set to about 2048 (any number near 2000 is fine).
24  * - If you receive a message saying the bridge could @e not be balanced,
25  * disconnect the entire Wheatstone bridge from the PolyDAQ and re-check the
26  * resistances with an ohmmeter. Note that using old, inexpensive 5%
27  * tolerance resistors to complete a quarter-bridge or half-bridge may
28  * cause the bridge to be too far out of balance to use with the
29  * auto-balancer; using a manually balanced channel 3 or 4 may solve this
30  * problem (or better yet, get more precisely toleranced resistors).
31  * - @b Manually @b balanced (strain channels 3 and 4):
32  * - Connect a voltmeter to measure the voltage between test point @b S3 or
33  * @b S4, at the edge of the circuit board, and @b GND.
34  * Use a tiny screwdriver to slowly rotate the adjustment screw on top of
35  * the bridge balancing potentiometer (a blue rectangular box) labeled
36  * @c BAL_3 or @c BAL_4 on the board. Adjust the potentiometer until the
37  * voltage between @b S3 or @b S4 and @b GND is about 1.67 volts.
38  * These are 15-turn trim potentiometers, so it may take several turns of
39  * the screwdriver.
40  * -# Calibrate the bridge as necessary for your application. This may be done by
41  * applying known loads and examining the resulting output, or by performing
42  * the appropriate calculations using the known gauge, amplifier, and A/D
43  * converter properties.
44  *
45  * @image html strain_bridge_connections_small.png
46  * @image latex strain_bridge_connections.png
47  *
48  * @subsection sg_details Details
49  * Strain gauges must be connected in a Wheatstone bridge circuit, and the bridge
50  * must be built entirely by the user. There are no brige completion resistors on
51  * the PolyDAQ board. Strain gauges and resistors of any resistance from 120 ohms
52  * to 1000 ohms may be used, as long as all the resistances are the same within a
53  * given bridge. 350 ohm gauges are preferred, as they give a good balance
54  * between low power usage (for which higher resistance gauges are better) and
55  * low noise (for which lower resistance gauges are better). If plain resistors
56  * are used to complete a quarter- or half-bridge, precision resistors of 1% or
57  * better tolerance are recommended.
58  *
59  * Before connecting a strain gauge bridge to the PolyDAQ 2, connect all four
60  * resistances in the bridge (be they active gauges, dummy gauges, or resistors)
61  * and use an ohmmeter to check the resistances between the corners of the bridge.
62  * The resistance between opposite bridge corners should be equal to the
63  * resistance of each gauge or resistor. The resistance between adjacent corners
64  * (across each one resistance) should be 3/4 of the resistance of one resistor
65  * when that resistor was out of the bridge. If the bridge resistances are not
66  * within 1% of the values they should have, @c do @c not connect the bridge to
67  * the PolyDAQ; it will be impossible to balance and won't work. Fix the bridge
68  * and \e then connect it. @b Note: Do not attempt to measure bridge resistances
69  * after connecting the bridge to the PolyDAQ board; always disconnect the bridge
70  * from the board before measuring resistances.
71  *
72  * The four "corners" of the bridge are connected to a set of four adjacent
73  * terminals @c VEX, @c Sn+, @c Sn-, and @c GND on the PolyDAQ. The @c VEX
74  * terminal is the excitation voltage, which is controlled by the board's
75  * onboard regulator at 3.3V. The @c Sn+ and @c Sn- terminals (replace the letter
76  * @c n with the bridge number) connect to the Wheatstone bridge's outputs, which
77  * are sometimes referred to as the bridge's @c A and @c B outputs on
78  * documentation. The @c GND connection is of course ground.
79  *
80  * @subsubsection sss_sg_bal Balancing Strain Gauge Bridges
81  * Each PolyDAQ 2 has two strain gauge bridges which can be automatically
82  * balanced. There are two auto-balanced bridges because the STM32F405VG
83  * microcontroller has two D/A converters which are used for the balancing.
84  * On boards with four bridge amplifiers, two of the bridges use manual balancing
85  * potentiometers which are discussed below.
86  *
87  * The @b auto-balancer can be operated from the PolyDAQ GUI or a terminal.
88  * The GUI is documented in its own section of this manual.
89  * When communicating with a PolyDAQ 2 through a dumb terminal, the strain bridge
90  * auto-balancer is invoked by the commands @b L and @b M for the first and
91  * second bridges respectively. A message will be shown in the terminal telling
92  * if the auto-balancing was successful. If there is a problem with the bridge
93  * resistances (usually, one resistance is missing or a connection is bad) the
94  * bridge cannot be balanced. The bridge must be disconnected from the PolyDAQ
95  * and re-checked with an ohmmeter. When the problem has been solved, reconnect
96  * the bridge and auto-balancing should be successful.
97  *
98  * The @b manual @b balancer is operated by turning the adjustment screws on
99  * each of the two bridge balancing potentiometers marked @c BAL_3 and @c BAL_4.
100  * The potentiometers are blue colored rectangular boxes near the @c S3 and
101  * @c S4 inputs on the terminal blocks. Use a voltmeter (@b not an oscilloscope)
102  * to measure the voltage at the @c S3 or @c S4 test point near the edge of the
103  * PolyDAQ board. With the strain gauge at its zero-strain or equilibrium
104  * position, turn the potentiometer screw for the appropriate channel so
105  * that the voltage at the @c S3 or @c S4 test point becomes about 1.67 volts.
106  * This will set the A/D output to about half its full scale so that both positive
107  * and negative strains can be measured. If you need the maximum range and
108  * resolution possible from the bridge amplifier, you can adjust the potentiometer
109  * so that the amplifier output voltage at the @c S3 or @c S4 test point is close
110  * to zero, though a zero-strain reading of exactly zero volts is not recommended
111  * because it will make a drift of the zero point to voltages below zero
112  * undetectable. After you've adjusted the gain potentiometer, apply some positive
113  * load to the device being tested (if possible) and verify that the amplifier
114  * output voltage is going in the right direction. If it is going the wrong way,
115  * reversing the Wheatstone bridge output connections to the @c Sn+ and @c Sn-
116  * terminals and re-zeroing the bridge will cause positive strains to register as
117  * positive voltages.
118  *
119  * @subsubsection sg_calib Calibration
120  * The excitation voltage for the strain gauges is nominally 3.3 volts.
121  * For the best accuracy, you can measure the excitation voltage when all your
122  * strain gauges are connected and the PolyDAQ 2 is using the same power supply
123  * (such as a particular laptop's USB connection) that is used during your tests.
124  * Make sure to use an accurate voltmeter set to the range which gives the most
125  * resolution. Each strain gauge bridge amplifier has a gain of
126  * 100.2 @plusminus 0.5%.
127  * The bridge output goes directly to the 12-bit A/D converter's input, and
128  * the A/D converter's reference voltage is supplied by a high-accuracy 3.3 volt
129  * reference source (LT1460) whose accuracy rating is better than 0.1%.
130  *
131  * @section sec_vin Voltage Inputs
132  *
133  * @subsection ss_vin_steps Steps
134  * -# Ensure that your voltage source produces at least 0.5 volts or so of output
135  * when your maximum signal is being measured and that it has a negative
136  * output or common ground that can be connected to the PolyDAQ's ground.
137  * -# Ensure that you can power the sensor from the PolyDAQ's 3.3 volt excitation
138  * voltage (@c VEX terminals). If not, find an appropriate power supply for
139  * your sensor. The power supply's ground must be attached to the PolyDAQ's
140  * ground. In any case, sensors whose output is greater than @plusminus 10
141  * volts should not be used.
142  * -# If the sensor's output will always be in the range of 0 -- 3.3 volts,
143  * connect the sensor's output to the board's @c V_3 or @c V_4 input if you
144  * have a PolyDAQ 0-4-4 board, or any voltage input for a PolyDAQ 4-4-2 board.
145  * -# If the sensor's output is larger but in the range of -10 to +10 volts,
146  * connect it to the @c V_1 or @c V_2 input of a PolyDAQ 0-4-4 board or any
147  * voltage input of a PolyDAQ 4-4-2 board.
148  * -# Check that the calibration of voltage inputs is correct using a high
149  * accuracy voltmeter (not the $4.00 kind).
150  *
151  * @subsection ss_vin_det Details
152  * The PolyDAQ 2 has two types of voltage inputs, with different configurations
153  * of the board using different combinations thereof:
154  * - @plusminus 10 volt inputs. These inputs use a voltage divider and level
155  * shifter circuit to convert signals in the range of approximately -10 to
156  * 10 volts into signals in the range 0 -- 3.3 volts, which are measured by
157  * the A/D converter. If the inputs are disconnected, the A/D converter will
158  * return a reading of about 2048, or halfway between the minimum and maximum.
159  * Connecting these inputs to ground should produce an A/D output of about 1726.
160  * - 0 -- 3.3 volt inputs. These inputs
161  * All the voltage inputs are single-ended; that is, all are referenced to ground.
162  * Differential voltage measurements must be made using an external differential
163  * amplifier or, for small enough differences, the strain gauge bridge amplifiers
164  * as described in the @ref sec_mV "Millivolt Inputs" section.
165  *
166  * For best accuracy, voltage inputs should be calibrated against a high-accuracy
167  * voltmeter. The resistors used to divide and shift @plusminus 10 volt signals
168  * into the 0 -- 3.3 volt signals read by the analog to digital converter are
169  * not especially high accuracy resistors, so the gains of individual PolyDAQ
170  * boards may vary by up to about 3%. The 0 -- 3.3V inputs should be accurate to
171  * within better than 0.5% as long as input impedance is not a problem.
172  *
173  * @plusminus 10 volt inputs have an input impedance of approximately 220K\ohms.
174  * This means that they can be used to measure signals from amplified transducers
175  * without loading the measured signals, but low-energy signals such as those
176  * from piezoelectric, capacitive, thermocouple, and biological sensors must be
177  * amplified by proper signal conditioning amplifiers before being sent to the
178  * PolyDAQ's voltage inputs.
179  *
180  * 0 -- 3.3 volt inputs have an input impedance of approximately 300K\ohms and
181  * are subject to the same considerations of source impedance as the @plusminus 10
182  * volt inputs. Although these inputs are somewhat protected from excessive
183  * voltages by series resistors at their inputs, care must be taken to prevent
184  * voltages outside the range 0 -- 3.3 volts from being connected to these
185  * inputs.
186  *
187  * @subsubsection sec_pot Potentiometers
188  * Using a potentiometer as a position transducer is especially easy.
189  * -# Choose a potentiometer whose resistance is 1K@ohms to 10K@ohms, and make
190  * sure it has a "linear taper" (linear relationship between angle or
191  * distance and resistance change).
192  * -# Connect one end of the potentiometer to the +3.3V source at a terminal
193  * marked @c VEX.
194  * -# Connect the other end of the potentiometer to a ground terminal.
195  * -# Connect the potentiometer's center terminal, the one for the moving "wiper",
196  * to a voltage input (on a PolyDAQ 0-4-4, inputs @c V_3 and @c V_4 will give
197  * the best resolution).
198  * -# Run the PolyDAQ GUI or a dumb terminal to test and calibrate the position
199  * to voltage transducer. See \ref pd_channels "Channel Commands" for a list
200  * of commands to get data from each A/D channel.
201  *
202  * @subsubsection sec_mV Millivolt Inputs
203  * If you need to measure a small difference between voltages which are both in
204  * the range 0 - 3.3V from ground, you can use a strain gauge bridge amplifier
205  * as a differential amplifier with a gain of about 100. It is best to protect
206  * the amplifier's inputs by placing 100\ohms resistors in series with the
207  * voltage source, unless the excitation voltage and ground are used to power
208  * the sensors (as they are with strain gauge bridges). If both inputs "float"
209  * with respect to ground (such as with self-powering sensors such as
210  * thermocouples), use a 100K\ohms to 1M\ohms resistor between the @c Sn- input
211  * and ground to ensure that the signals both remain within the measurable
212  * range of 0 to 3.3 volts.
